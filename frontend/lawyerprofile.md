"I once knew someone on a drafting team at a firm who filed two provisional patent applications in his daughter's name just to help her get admission to a university in the US. Those applications were later abandoned."

This is what a IP lawyer told me while I was trying to make sense of the data. A recently released response in the Lok Sabha reveals that startups filed 13,089 patent applications between FY21 and FY25. 

Yet only 2,174 of those patents were eventually granted, and nearly 500 applications were abandoned or withdrawn at early procedural stages, often before any substantive examination by the patent office.

The numbers also expose a broader structural mismatch between startup enthusiasm and the system’s capacity to process filings. Of the 13,089 startup patent applications, only 4,172 have even been examined so far, just 32%. The backlog has grown as India’s overall patent filings nearly doubled between FY21 and FY25, while examinations dropped sharply.

Most paralegals don’t know they can do this… but it changed everything for me.

When I first started, I thought my job was to just wait for direction.
Wait for an assignment.
Wait for an update.
Wait for the attorney to remember what needed to happen next.

One day, a deadline almost slipped through the cracks — not because I didn’t know about it, but because I assumed, “Well… the attorney will mention it.”

They didn’t.

And something in me said: “No. Speak up.”

So I walked into their office and said, “Hey, that motion deadline is coming up. Do you want me to draft the affirmation so we’re ahead of it?”

The look on their face? Relief.
Not annoyance.
Not frustration.
Relief.

That’s when I realized the secret nobody tells new paralegals:

You’re not just assisting. You’re managing UP.
If the attorney forgets something?
If something’s overdue?
If something feels unclear or off?

You CAN — and SHOULD — speak up.

Ever since then, I’ve treated every case like a shared responsibility. I keep the team updated, flag issues early, and follow up even when it’s uncomfortable.

And here’s the truth:

Law offices don’t fall apart because of a lack of knowledge.
They fall apart because of silence.

Proactive communication is what keeps cases — and teams — together.

That day taught me that being a strong paralegal isn’t about knowing everything…
It’s about never being afraid to say something when it matters.… more

His Views on The Legal System in Goa
Contrary to popular belief, Borkar believes that the justice system in Goa can be improved not by putting more pressure on the judiciary but by reducing their workload. He feels that if hearings that don't require the application of judicial mind are taken up before registrars or superintendents, up to 1/3rd of court time could be better utilised. He suggests that a judge should preside over a court for only half-day. freeing up the rest of their day for reading and writing judgments. This will double the number of
judges within the same infrastructure and radically bring down pendency.
His Advice To Young Lawyers
Aspiring lawyers and law students can learn valuable lessons from Borkar's work ethic, zeal for learning and empathy towards clients. He encourages youngsters to explore various fields before honing their expertise in areas that resonate with their passions. He feels that lawyers should take pride in their work, as it makes them want to improve continuously. He believes that a healthy work-life halance is very important and that lawyers, especially young lawyers, while working hard in the profession, should not neglect their physical and mental well- being and their family.
